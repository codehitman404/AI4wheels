Driving AI Adoption in Organizations: What We Learned from 20+ Interviews

Published on April 22, 2025

Our team at Georgetown set out to understand how organizations are navigating the journey of AI adoption. Through a blend of primary interviews and secondary research, we captured key lessons, challenges, and success strategies that are shaping the future of intelligent enterprise.

🎯 The Objective

We interviewed over 20 professionals from across industries—automotive, healthcare, finance, and technology. These individuals represented functions ranging from software engineering and customer service to senior management. Our goal: identify what works, what doesn’t, and what’s next for AI at work.

📊 Key Findings

  • Productivity Gains: AI is improving coding efficiency by up to 40% in software roles.
  • Organic Adoption: Employees often start using AI tools independently before formal rollout.
  • Custom Metrics: Each function needs its own way to measure AI’s success.
  • Data Privacy Concerns: 63% of leaders cite data governance as a key barrier.

🧠 The Power of Personas

We categorized users into four types: Worker-User, Worker-Non-User, Management-User, and Management-Non-User. Each group had different motivations and resistance points—allowing us to build more tailored strategies.

"I use AI for brainstorming, but I’ll never trust it in a final decision." — Interviewee, Creative Team Lead

🔧 Strategy Implications

  • Short Term: Tailored AI training, AI peer groups, and executive workshops.
  • Long Term: Internal AI taskforces, performance-linked adoption rewards, and ethical oversight boards.

📈 Use Cases Worth Watching

From AI-powered reconciliation in finance, to automated customer support in airlines, real-world applications show measurable impact. Microsoft Copilot and SkodaGPT are being used to streamline operations, summarize financial data, and automate routine reporting.

🚧 Challenges Ahead

Resistance isn't just about fear of job loss—it’s about trust. Output inaccuracy, unclear benefits, and data concerns remain the biggest obstacles.
